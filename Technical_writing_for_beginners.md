# Beginner’s Guide to Writing Technical Content That Non-Tech Readers Understand






**Clear writing beats Clever writing every time.**

[Technical writing](https://medium.com/@bevavoawe/beginners-guide-to-writing-technical-content-that-non-tech-readers-understand-71b90cd772ec)


Have you ever tried reading an article, but found it difficult to understand what you were reading?

How did that make you feel? Frustrated, right?

Exactly! That’s how non-technical readers feel when they struggle to understand your technical content. It’s almost like you are talking to yourself — and of course, that’s not what you want.

If you are an aspiring technical writer, a general writer trying to switch into technical writing, or a developer who wants to explain their product better to non-technical users, this guide will help you learn how to write your content to reach the heart of your desired audience.

Consider these **seven** tips when writing:

### Know your Audience First
Before writing, it is essential to know who your audience is. So, ask yourself this important question, “*Who am I writing for?*”

There are several quick ways to determine who they are:

**1. Start with the Title**

Imagine you are writing on this topic, “*A Beginner’s Guide to Understanding Variables in Python*”. Based on the title, you can identify who your target audience should be.

Now, let’s analyze it:

The term “**Beginner**” signals that the article is meant for people with little or no prior experience in Python. This could include:

- Students who are in their early years of studying computer science or related fields.
- Self-learners who are new to coding and have just picked up Python.
- Professionals from non-technical backgrounds exploring programming for the first time.
- Hobbyists who are curious about programming but have no prior exposure.
  
By analyzing the title, you would be able to determine some of your target audiences. This makes it easy to tailor your content to suit them better.

**2.  Look at the platform you are publishing on**

The platform you are publishing your content on is another way to determine your audience:

- If you’re writing on **Medium**, your readers may be learners, hobbyists, or professionals who are looking for simple explanations.
- If it’s a **developer’s documentation** on GitHub, your readers are more likely to be programmers.
- If you’re writing for a **company blog**, your audience might include potential customers or non-technical users who need plain language.
 ### Use Simple Language
As a technical writer, your goal is to communicate with your audience in the clearest way possible; therefore, avoid using complex or complicated terms when simpler words will suffice.

[Use simple language](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*YvtIYx4o3zn5YEmsmw1cww.png)

If you must use a technical term, define it right away so your readers don’t get lost.

For Example, a non-technical reader may not know what an API(Application Programming Interface) is, so you need to explain terms like this.

### Be Consistent
It’s easy to overlook consistency because you are the one doing the writing, and you understand what you mean. But your readers don’t live in your head, so if you switch between terms, acronyms, or commands, they can quickly get confused.


For example:

- Don’t write “**Hypertext Markup Language**” in one place and later switch to “**HTML**” without telling readers that both mean the same thing.
- If you use “**command line**” at first, don’t suddenly change to “**terminal**” unless you’ve explained that they’re the same.
  
The rule is simple: pick one version, introduce it clearly, and stick to it throughout your document.

This way, your readers don’t waste energy wondering whether you’re introducing something new when you’re really just renaming the same thing.

### Prefer Active Voice Over Passive
Try to use active voice most of the time and use passive voice only when necessary. Active voice makes your writing clearer and easier to read. Here’s why:

- Readers usually change passive sentences into active ones in their heads, which takes extra effort.
- Passive voice makes sentences confusing and less direct.
- Sometimes, passive sentences don’t say who is doing the action, which leaves the reader guessing.
- Active voice is usually shorter and more straightforward.
  
So, keep it simple — use active voice!

For Example:

**Active voice:**

*The developer fixed the bug in the code*.

**Passive voice:**

*The bug in the code was fixed by the developer*.

Notice how the **active voice** is shorter and direct, while the **passive voice** sounds longer and less clear.
### Use task-based headings
Choose a heading that describes the task your reader should work on; headings should help readers to quickly find the action they need to take. So, instead of naming a tool or using unfamiliar jargon, write your heading around the task the reader wants to complete.

For Example:

Let’s say you are documenting the process of setting up a website to go live. To do this, the reader needs to configure Nginx. A more natural way to write this heading would be:

- Configuring Nginx.
For a seasoned developer, this might make sense, but for beginners or non-technical readers, it can be confusing. Use a task-based heading like this instead:

- Set Up Your Website to go live.
### Use Visuals and Examples
Visuals can express ideas in ways that words alone sometimes can’t. They make complex ideas easier for readers to understand. For example, instead of writing long sentences to explain what a user interface is, you can show a screenshot.

If you’re writing about code, an illustration of the system’s architecture might help readers understand how different parts work together.

Therefore, as a Technical Writer, it is vital to know how to use Visuals to your advantage.

And, of course, **examples!** Using examples is a great way to help your readers. So, provide examples that are relevant to what you are discussing. That drives the point home and makes it easier for readers to understand you.

### Test with a Non-Tech Reader
Before publishing your content, it is best to test it. Think of it like an app before it is launched. Developers don’t just release it straight to users — they ask testers (who weren’t involved in building it) to try it out. Why? Because fresh eyes can catch bugs and usability issues that the creators missed. The same applies to your writing. Have a non-technical reader review your content to ensure it’s clear and easy to follow.

## Conclusion
Great technical writing isn’t about showing how much you know — it’s about helping others understand. By knowing your audience, keeping your language simple, staying consistent, using active voice, creating task-based headings, adding visuals and examples, and testing with non-technical readers, you can make your content clear and more impactful. Remember, when your readers understand you easily, you’ve not just written content— you’ve created value.
